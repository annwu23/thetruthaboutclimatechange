Causes
  1. Carbon Emissions

    Contrary to popular belief, carbon dioxide is actually very important for the environment. It makes up a small percent of the atmosphere, and it traps the sun's rays inside the atmosphere. This makes our planet warmer, an effect known as the "greenhouse effect", and we might have all died of cold if carbon dioxide were nonexistent.
    So, what's the problem? In recent years, we've pumped out more carbon dioxide from our factories and cars than the environment can handle — in fact, we humans have increased carbon dioxide by a third since the Industrial Revolution. This extra carbon dioxide joins the original carbon dioxide, heating up the planet more. This heat, in turn, can disrupt the balance of the ecosystem by melting important glaciers and making it harder for heat-intolerant species to survive… and prompting us to use more air conditioning, which gives off more CO2. CO2 (the shorthand for carbon dioxide), then, is a good thing, but only in small amounts.

  2. Other Molecules

    What about other gases, though? Although CO2 is the most well-known, many other gases contribute to climate change:
    • Methane — This greenhouse gas is actually more active and potent than carbon dioxide is (34 times), but there is a much smaller amount of it in the atmosphere. Methane, or CH4, can be found in landfills, agriculture (especially rice agriculture), and livestock (such as cattle).
    • Nitrous oxide — This is an EXTREMELY potent greenhouse gas, 298 times more powerful than carbon dioxide. Nitrous oxide (N2O) also destroys the ozone layer, which helps protect from the sun's UV (ultraviolet) rays. Luckily, this gas is much scarcer than carbon dioxide.
    • Water vapor — Water vapor, with its well-known shorthand of H2O, seems to have nothing to do with climate change. However, it does act as a greenhouse gas — and, more importantly, a feedback system. What does that mean? More water evaporates when the climate warms. This means that when carbon dioxide goes up, the amount of water vapor goes up as well, making the climate even hotter.

  3. Human Activities

    These gas emissions aren't the only cause of climate change; other activities that wouldn't exist without humans are also harming the environment. These are just two examples:

    • Deforestation — A person chopping down a tree is a common symbol for not caring about the environment, and for a good reason. Forests are terrifically important for the environment: they provide shelter for many animals (deers, bears, squirrels…) and, more importantly for climate change, absorb huge amounts of carbon dioxide and give out oxygen. However, as trees disappear from being used as timber or carelessly burned down by forest fires, carbon dioxide increases in the atmosphere.
    • Agriculture — Agriculture is a huge producer of methane and nitrous oxide, two greenhouse gases mentioned above. Methane comes from livestock's belches/digestion process and the uneaten food we dump in landfills, and nitrous oxide can come from the organic and mineral nitrogen fertilizer.
