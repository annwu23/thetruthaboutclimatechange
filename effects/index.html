Effects
  1. Temperature Rise

    Of course, at the rate at which carbon dioxide is pumping out of our factories and cars, our Earth is getting hotter and hotter. This change isn't uniform, as many like to think: because human-caused warming is added to the naturally varying climate, different places will still have different temperatures. (Some places might even still remain "cold", which is why the term "global warming" has mostly been jettisoned for "climate change".) However, the temperature on average is in fact rising, and it only takes a little push to start a chain of dominoes.

  2. Sea Rise

    Sea rise is directly linked to temperature rise; it's the second domino in the chain, one might say. Glaciers and massive icebergs, previously considered invulnerable, are being weakened more and more from climate change. Antarctica, that huge expanse of ice and snow, is slowly melting, and the Arctic might even be completely ice-free by 2040. (This is because Antarctica is mostly on land, whereas the Arctic is in the sea, making it more vulnerable to the rising sea temperatures.)

  3. Extreme Weather

    How can weather be related to climate change? Well, let's first turn to the storms that have been causing the most havoc recently (as of Oct. 2017): hurricanes (or, technically, "tropical cyclones"). Hurricanes originate from the sea: the warm, moist air rises, new air slides into the bottom, the new air is heated, it rises… and the cycle keeps going, all fed from the warm sea. Climate change, though, makes that sea warmer, and thus the hurricanes become (even more) intense.
    What else? Heat, a huge effect of climate change. Because of increased heat, heat waves are getting more frequent. Some may be aware of the devastating heat wave in Europe that killed 70,000 people in 2003. In fact, that heat wave killed 506 people in Paris alone. This event should be so rare that it happens once in about 500 years; studies have found that that number has crashed down to 40.
    Finally, climate change can make both droughts and floods more extreme. Climate change sucks moisture from the land and seas into the air; when there's no rain, this worsens droughts, and when there is rain (or snow), it worsens floods (such as the 2016 ones in Houston and Paris).

  4. Species Extinction

    Much like the bobcat in this picture, many species are going endangered or even extinct due to climate change. Here are just a few examples:

    • Red knot — This bird must fly 18,000 miles (round-trip) each year, along one of the longest migratory routes in the animal kingdom. It stands to reason that, while traveling, they have to stop at some points to rest and hunt. Red knots usually rest near the coastline, but that rest stop is shrinking because of sea rise, which (as mentioned above) comes with climate change as a package deal.
    • Sea stars — These overlooked animals are actually a very important link in the sea ecosystem. Some may know that in 2013, there was a huge die-off of sea stars. The population is slowly recovering from the disease that caused it — Sea Star Wasting Syndrome, or SSWS. However, one study suggested that the reason the disease was so harmful was because of the sea warming, which is of course related to climate change.
    • Caribou — A food source for many people, especially native tribes in Northern Canada, caribou numbers are going down. Why? Climate change is threatening their habitat: the forests of the Northwest Territories. The Canadian Nature Conservancy is working hard to create a National Park reserve that will be 3.5 acres of protection for caribou.
